Does AI Have a Sense of Humor?
Measuring Systematic Bias in LLM Joke Preferences

Millán González-Bueno Aguirre, Zuzana Navratil, Alice Nguyen, Barthélemy Reboul, Mathias Setterblad

We present a tournament-style experiment measuring whether Claude (Anthropic) exhibits systematic bias when judging the humor of jokes across nine social categories. Annotating 10,000 Reddit jokes and running 80,000+ pairwise judgments, we uncover a strong category bias (Class/Occupation wins 66%, Sexual Orientation wins 38%), a robust recency bias (position-2 chosen 65% of the time), and a previously unreported interaction between the two. Joke length is ruled out as a confound.

01

Research Question

Large language models are now routinely used as evaluators: they rank essays, summarise conversations, score creative writing, and recommend content. Unlike generation tasks, evaluation tasks expose the model's implicit preferences — which may encode biases that are invisible in standard benchmarks.

Humor is a particularly revealing test case. It is culturally situated, socially sensitive, and highly subjective — yet it allows for clean experimental control (pairwise comparisons, randomised positions, factorial category design). If a model systematically favors jokes about certain social groups while downgrading others, this constitutes a measurable, consequential bias.

Central question: Does Claude exhibit systematic, category-level bias when asked to evaluate the humor of jokes targeting different social groups? And if so, does this bias interact with known positional artifacts such as recency bias?

Hypotheses

H1 — Category bias. Win rates across the nine categories will differ significantly from the 50% baseline expected under a fair evaluator (χ² test, Wilson 95% CIs).

H2 — Recency bias. The model will show a significant positional preference, choosing the second-presented joke more often than the first across all matchups.

H3 — Interaction. Categories with strong inherent preference will resist the recency disadvantage when placed in position 1, producing a detectable category × position interaction.

02

Key Findings

Three main results emerge from 36 head-to-head matchups and over 80,000 pairwise judgments. Full analysis and figures are on the Results page.

66%
Win rate · Class/Occupation

The strongest category beats opponents in two-thirds of all matchups — 16 points above the fair-evaluator baseline.

38%
Win rate · Sexual Orientation

The weakest category: loses more often than any other group, 12 points below baseline.

65%
Recency bias · position 2

Across all matchups, the model selects the second joke 65% of the time — a highly significant positional artifact.

↕
Interaction effect

Strong categories resist positional disadvantage: Class/Occupation at position 1 still wins 54% vs. only 21% for Sexual Orientation.
