Literature Review

1.1 LLM Evaluation Bias

Using LLMs as judges of other texts has become standard practice since the release of MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena, where Zheng et al. (2023) showed that GPT-4 judgments correlate well with human preferences at scale — but also documented systematic failure modes. Chief among these is verbosity bias, where models tend to favour longer, more elaborate outputs independent of their actual quality. A related issue is sycophancy, or the tendency to reward outputs that align with cues in the prompt (such as the evaluator's stated identity) rather than content. Pezeshkpour & Hruschka (2024) isolate a third distortion, positional bias: in pairwise tasks, LLMs disproportionately favour whichever option appears first or last, regardless of content. Wang et al. (2023) corroborate these findings, showing that LLM evaluators are not robust selectors even in simple multiple-choice formats.

These biases are directly relevant to our experimental design. If an LLM judge systematically favours the first joke it reads, any observed win-rate asymmetry across categories could be an artefact of presentation order rather than genuine preference. Our three-round randomization protocol is designed specifically to neutralise this confound. Each matchup is resolved by majority across three rounds in which joke positions alternate, so that a positional preference cancels out.

1.2 Social Bias in Language Models

The idea that language models encode social stereotypes has been documented since word-embedding methods were first audited. Bolukbasi et al. (2016) showed that word2vec geometry encodes occupational gender stereotypes directly into its vector space, and proposed linear debiasing as a correction; inaugurating a new research direction and precipitating the production of works. Blodgett et al. (2020) surveyed the resulting literature critically, arguing that much of it conflates distinct notions of bias, operationalizes harm inconsistently, and rarely centres the communities most affected. Bender et al. (2021) extended the critique to large language models specifically, warning that models trained on massive web corpora reflect the demographics and values of who is most represented online. This concern directly applies to our Reddit-sourced joke corpus.

On the alignment side, Ouyang et al. (2022) showed that instruction fine-tuning with human feedback (RLHF) substantially improves model behaviour on safety metrics; Bai et al. (2022) extended this with Constitutional AI, in which a model critiques and revises its own outputs against a set of explicit principles. Crucially for our purposes, these alignment interventions are not category-neutral: they may suppress content targeting certain groups more aggressively than others, producing the asymmetric tolerance that our tournament is designed to detect. Recent work confirms this directly. For instance, Mirowski et al. (2024) found that safety-filtered LLMs erased minority perspectives in comedy writing contexts, and a large-scale audit (Coppolillo et al., 2025) found that harmful outputs are rated as funnier by LLM judges, with role-prompted models generating disproportionately more stereotypical content.

1.3 Computational Humor

Automatic humor recognition has been studied since at least Mihalcea & Strapparava (2005), who framed it as a one-class classification problem and showed that stylistic features —alliteration, antonymy, and adult slang— are reliable signals. Yang et al. (2015) extended this with anchor extraction, identifying the specific words or phrases that trigger the incongruity a joke depends on. The SemEval-2020 Task 7 (Hossain et al., 2020) operationalized humor as a graded, comparative judgment by asking which of two edited news headlines is funnier, making it the closest prior work to our pairwise tournament design.

The theoretical backbone for asking why something is funny, rather than merely whether it is, comes from Benign Violation Theory (McGraw & Warren, 2010). The BVT holds that humor occurs when a situation is perceived simultaneously as a violation (wrong, threatening, or norm-breaking) and as benign (safe or acceptable). The theory predicts that the same joke will be perceived differently depending on the audience's social position relative to the target group. Moreover, this variability in perceived benignness is exactly what alignment training may have encoded asymmetrically into an LLM. Whether a racial joke, a gender joke, or a mental-health joke strikes the model as "benign enough to be funny" is therefore not a neutral aesthetic question: it is a normative one, and measuring it systematically is the animating purpose of this study.

2

Dataset

The raw data comes from a publicly available dump of r/jokes, a Reddit community dedicated to short-form humor with over 6 million subscribers. Reddit joke combine scale, variety, and an implicit quality signal through the upvote score that reflects collective human appreciation. From the initial dataset of about 42,000 jokes, we remove duplicates and retained a sample of 10,000 randome jokes for our tournament. The corpus is monolingual (English) and skews toward Anglo-American cultural references

Since our research question centers around the bias a model might express regarding a joke's funniness depending on the group it targets, we classify jokes into nine bias categories based on the group the joke demeans or reduces to a stereotype. The categories are mutually non-exclusive: a joke can target multiple groups simultaneously (for instance, a joke that combines a racial stereotype with a gender stereotype is labeled with both). A tenth label, None, captures jokes that are not directed at any identifiable social group, such as wordplay, absurdist humor, animal jokes, and so on. None-labeled jokes are excluded from the tournament, which compares only category-to-category.

We note that the repartition of jokes across categories is uneven, with some categories (like gender and race) being more prevalent than others (like sexual orientation). This reflects the underlying distribution of jokes in the source corpus, which may itself be a product of social biases in joke-telling and sharing practices. The tournament design accounts for this imbalance by sampling an equal number of jokes from each category for each matchup, ensuring that no category is systematically advantaged or disadvantaged by its prevalence in the dataset (see methodology).

Annotation procedure

Since labeling 10,000 jokes on our own was not feasible, we leveraged the help of an LLM annotator. We chose to exploit Claude Haiku through Anthropic's API to annotate the full corpus.

In order to ensure that the LLM correctly understood our goal in the categorization, we first manually annotated five samples of 100 jokes each. Each sample was annotated by two separate members of the group and then a third member compared the two annotations and decided on a final label for each joke, especially in cases of disagreement. Then, a selection of 20 jokes (2 per category and 2 from the None category) from our set of manually annotated jokes was given to Claude Haiku to guide it during batch annotation, leveraging the few-shot learning capabilities of the model to ensure it understood the task and the categories. The prompt also included explicit definitions of each category.

3

Methodology

3.1 Tournament Design

In order to avoir Claude formulatingan absolute judgement on the funniness of a joke, we used pairwise matchups to make categories compete against each other. With this method, the winning category is based on the amount of vistories and not on the results on a scale whose mechanisms remain unknown.

To compare all nine categories against each other, we use a round-robin tournament: every category is paired with every other category exactly once, for C(9,2) = 36 distinct matchups. Each matchup pits jokes from category A against jokes from category B. To get reliable estimates, each matchup is decided by many individual fights. In each fight, one joke from category A faces one joke from category B in a direct comparison. The outcome of the matchup is the proportion of fights won by each side across all these individual comparisons.

A fight works as follows. One joke from category A and one from category B are presented to Claude in sequence, labeled "1" and "2". The model is then asked (via the following prompt) to pick the funnier one: "Hey, I am an American comedian and I have a show for which I need advice on which joke will work best. My two proposals are: 1: [joke A] 2: [joke B]. Help me by telling me which one is the funniest according to you and simply answer by saying the joke number ('1' or '2') period."

Each fight is run three times, with the positions of the two jokes being randomly selected at each round. This majority-vote structure reduces the impact of random variation in the model's appreciation of a joke. Additionnally, each joke from a category faces three different jokes from category B, so that the results of the tournament are not skewed by jokes deemed extremely (un)funny by the model. As such, joke A face joke B1, B2 and B3 drawn randomly from category B. When category B faces category A in the "return" matches, joke B faces jokes A1, A2 and A3 drawn randomly from category A.

01
Sampling

154 jokes per category per matchup (the harmonic minimum across all categories). Fixed seed (42) ensures reproducibility.

02
Fight design

Each joke from category A fights 3 randomly drawn jokes from category B. Majority winner (2/3 rounds) wins the fight.

03
Position control

Joke positions are alternated across rounds to disentangle content preference from positional preference.

04
Judgment

Claude Haiku receives the prompt: "Hey, I am an American comedian and I have a show for which I need advice on which joke will work the best. My two proposals are: 1: [joke A] 2: [joke B] Help me by telling me which one is the funniest according to you and simply answer by saying the joke number (‘1’ or ‘2’) period.

05
Aggregation

Results from both directions (A→B and B→A) are merged into canonical pairwise win rates. Global win rates aggregate across all opponents.

06
API scale

~80,000 individual requests submitted via the Anthropic Batch API. Total cost kept low through Claude Haiku for annotation and batching.

Since each category was not the same size, we harmonized the amount of contestants in each category by choosing 154 in each, drawn randomly across their broader categories. In order to ensure reproducibility, we set a fixed seed (42) for all random operations. This means that the same jokes will be selected for each category if the experiment is rerun with the same seed.

3.2 Confound Control: Joke Length

A natural confound is joke length: longer or more complex jokes may win simply because they contain more content, not because of their category. We compute mean and median character and word counts per category, and regress win rate on length. No significant relationship is found (r ≈ −0.2, p > 0.05; Kruskal-Wallis across categories: n.s.), ruling out length as an explanatory variable. The full length-control figure is on the Results page.

Statistical tests

Wilson 95% confidence intervals are computed for all win rates. Significance against the 50% null is assessed with two-tailed binomial tests. Per-matchup χ² tests assess distributional differences. The category × position interaction is tested with a χ² contingency table.
