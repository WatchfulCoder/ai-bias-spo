Discussion

Why does Class/Occupation dominate?

The most striking finding of this study is that Class/Occupation jokes win 66% of all pairwise matchups: 16 percentage points above the fair-evaluator baseline. Two complementary mechanisms plausibly explain this dominance, and disentangling them requires careful reflection on what "funny" means in this context.

The first explanation is structural. Class/Occupation humor tends to rely on wordplay, role reversals, and incongruity between social expectation and professional reality. These are precisely the stylistic features that Mihalcea & Strapparava (2005) identified as reliable signals of funniness: alliteration, antonymy, and the formal markers of joke structure. Crucially, this category's transgressiveness is typically low: making fun of an occupation or an activity does not implicate a protected characteristic in the way that racial or sexual-orientation humor does. According to Benign Violation Theory (McGraw & Warren, 2010), humor arises at the intersection of a perceived violation and benignness. In our case, the "benign" side of the equation is far easier to satisfy for occupational jokes than for jokes targeting minority identities. The model, in effect, applies a lower harmfulness discount to this category, allowing the underlying comedic quality of the joke to pass through more cleanly.

The second explanation operates at the level of training data. Large language models trained on web-scale datasets reflect the demographics and cultural production of those most represented online. Workplace and class-based humor is arguably the most pervasive and socially sanctioned form of comedy in Anglo-American internet culture, saturating memes, LinkedIn satire, and subreddits alike. A model whose pretraining corpus is saturated with this style may have acquired a structural affinity for its cadence and punchline logic. This hypothesis is harder to assess directly in a black-box API setting, but it aligns with the broader concern raised by Blodgett et al. (2020) that LLMs encode the preferences and blind spots of their training populations rather than offering neutral judgment.

Anthropic's own public documentation on Claude's character and values reinforces the first mechanism. Constitutional AI is explicitly designed to make the model refuse or downgrade content perceived as harmful, and the harm threshold is calibrated against characteristics protected under anti-discrimination law, which does not include class or occupation. The result is a hierarchy of tolerance that was designed for generation tasks but that is being shed the spotlight one during evaluation tasks.

Why does Sexual Orientation lose?

At the other end of the ranking, Sexual Orientation jokes win only 38% of matchups — the lowest of all nine categories and right behind Racial/Ethnic. This outcome is the mirror image of Class/Occupation's dominance and, we argue, flows from the same underlying cause: Anthropic's alignment interventions are not category-neutral.

The practical consequence is that a model asked to evaluate whether a joke is "funny" will implicitly route that judgment through a harm filter. For Sexual Orientation content, the filter is seemingly particularly aggressive. Even in a comparative context where the model is not being asked to generate harmful material but merely to assess relative comedic quality, the suppression mechanism appears to activate. The model declines to "reward" these jokes even in a framework that does not require it to endorse them.

This observation resonates with Mirowski et al. (2024), who found that safety-filtered LLMs erased minority perspectives in comedy writing, and with Coppolillo et al. (2025), who documented that LLM judges apply asymmetric standards across identity categories. The tension this creates is significant: a model deployed as a neutral evaluator of creative content cannot simultaneously be maximally safe and maximally impartial. Safety alignment, by design, encodes a normative stance that eventually reflects in preference scores.

Lastly, a further methodological factor may compound this effect. In our annotation procedure, Sexual Orientation was the most underrepresented category in the corpus, constituting only approximately 3% of annotated jokes. When Claude Haiku annotated the full dataset and our few-shot examples were weighted accordingly, the model may have inferred that Sexual Orientation humor represents a marginal, non-mainstream form of comedy. In the absence of strong positive training signal for this category, the safety-suppression effect operates almost unchallenged. The annotation dependency identified in our limitations section thus amplifies the very bias the experiment was designed to detect.

Recency bias as an attention artifact

The 65% position-2 preference we document is large, consistent, and present in every one of the 36 matchups. It replicates and extends the findings Pezeshkpour & Hruschka (2024), who showed that option order distorts LLM judgment in multiple-choice settings.

The most plausible mechanistic account is attentional: transformer architectures weight recent tokens more heavily through the key-value attention mechanism, an effect sometimes called "lost in the middle" (Liu et al., 2023). When two jokes are presented sequentially, the second joke occupies a more salient position in the context window at the moment the model generates its verdict. This is not a deliberate preference but a structural artifact of how attention is computed over long sequences. The implications are non-trivial: any evaluation task in which option order is not counterbalanced will systematically advantage whatever appears last.

Our design did not include "corrective" prompts precising that the order should not be considered as relevant for the evaluation. This means our recency bias estimate is likely an upper bound on what would be observed in a more carefully engineered evaluation pipeline. But, the randomization process we implemented undoubtedly helped the protocol to converge towards an unbiased situation.

Limitations

  • 🤖
    Single model, single snapshot. All judgments were made by Claude Haiku, a version specifically designed for the API. Results may not generalise to other LLMs (GPT-4o, Gemini, Llama 3) or to future releases of the same model after further safety tuning. Similarly, large-scale category labels were produced by a version of Claude Sonnet, creating a circular dependency: the same model family annotates and judges. This is likely to impact the relevance of the outcomes, even thought the API it is important to underscores that the API remains completly independent of the LLM. The five human-annotated validation samples provide partial coverage only. Therefore, our results shall be only interprated at the light of a specific and non-exhaustive framework, and shall not be extended to other models.
  • 🌍
    Dataset scope and representativeness. The Reddit joke corpus skews toward English-language, culturally Western humor, produced by a non-representative internet population. Moreover, category proportions may not reflect real-world distributions as internauts arguably do not behave and share content the same way online and offline.
  • 📊
    Statistical power per cell. With 154 jokes per side per matchup, some pairwise estimates carry wide confidence intervals. Small within-matchup effects may remain underpowered.
3

Conclusion

We set out to ask whether a state-of-the-art language model judges humor fairly across social categories. The answer is unambiguously no. Two systematic biases coexist: a category bias (a 28-point spread from 66% to 38% win rate) and a recency bias (position-2 preferred in 65% of fights). Crucially, these are not independent: strong categories partially resist positional disadvantage, revealing a hierarchy of preferences that overrides low-level attention artifacts.

Joke length is ruled out as a confound, and the effects are consistent and statistically robust across 36 matchups and 80,000+ individual judgments.

The practical implication is that LLM-based evaluators should not be deployed as neutral arbiters of creative content without explicit bias auditing. Tournament-style experiments, cheap to run at scale with modern batch APIs, offer a tractable framework for such audits. Future work should replicate these results across models and languages, investigate the safety-alignment origins of category biases, and explore debiasing strategies such as position shuffling, multi-model juries, and chain-of-thought elicitation.
